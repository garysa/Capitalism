# Discussion Topics Index

A structured map of the topics covered in this analysis.

---

## 1. Industrial Capitalism vs Financial Capitalism

**Core distinction:**
- **Industrial capitalism** — capital deployed to produce goods, build
  infrastructure, generate employment, expand productive capacity
- **Financial capitalism** — capital deployed to generate returns through
  financial instruments, speculation, credit creation, asset appreciation

**Key question:** Do both need to coexist in one system?

**China's answer:** Developmental state capitalism — state-directed industrial
policy with controlled financial system. Long time horizons via 5-year plans.
Capital subordinated to productive goals.

**Western trajectory:** Post-1980s shift toward financialization. Finance as
the dominant sector extracting value from the productive economy rather than
enabling it.

---

## 2. China's Developmental State Model

**Key features:**
- State ownership of strategic banks — credit directed to strategic priorities
- Industrial policy (Made in China 2025) — sector targets set at state level
- Capital account restrictions — insulates from financial speculation
- Long planning horizons — absorbs short-term losses for strategic gain
- Political primacy over capital — Jack Ma suppression as symbol

**Concrete outcomes:**
- ~30% of global manufacturing output
- >80% of global solar panel production
- BYD overtook Tesla in EV sales
- CATL holds ~37% global EV battery market
- More high-speed rail than rest of world combined
- ~50% of global shipbuilding

**Historical precedent:** South Korea, Japan, Taiwan — all used developmental
state model to industrialize. China doing it at unprecedented scale.

## 3. The Dollar System

**Current status:** Slow structural decline, not acute collapse.

**Pressures:**
- US debt-to-GDP ~125% and rising
- Dollar share of global reserves fell from ~70% (2000) to ~58% (2024)
- BRICS payment rail construction
- Saudi Arabia accepting yuan for oil
- Interest on US debt exceeds defence spending
- Dollar weaponisation (Iran, Russia sanctions) accelerating alternatives

**Exorbitant privilege:** US borrows in own currency, exports inflation,
runs permanent deficits — this is the structural benefit of reserve status
and the primary mechanism of US financial power projection.

**Petrodollar:** 1974 Nixon-Kissinger deal — Saudi Arabia prices oil in dollars,
recycles surpluses into US Treasuries. MBS accepting yuan breaks this directly.

## 4. Financial Capitalism as Rent-Seeking

**Definition of rent-seeking:** Extracting value from existing wealth rather
than creating new productive capacity.

**The FIRE economy (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate):**
- Finance share of US corporate profits: ~10% (1980) → ~40% (2024)
- Most financial products are zero-sum redistribution
- High-frequency trading extracts from price discovery without contributing
- Private equity: leverage + fees + asset stripping = value extraction
- Share buybacks vs capital investment — financial engineering over production

**Key theorists:**
- Michael Hudson — FIRE economy as parasitic on productive economy
- Mariana Mazzucato — value extraction vs value creation distinction
- Henry George — land rent as original rent-seeking (1879, still relevant)
- Steve Keen — private debt as economic instability driver

## 5. Can the West Reform Without Collapse?

**Historical pattern:** Major structural reform almost always required crisis,
war, or near-collapse.

**Examples of reform requiring crisis:**
- New Deal — required Great Depression
- Bretton Woods — required WWII devastation
- Post-war welfare states — required war destruction
- Nordic model — required Depression + labour conflict

**The 2008 test case — and failure:**
- Perfect conditions for reform — visible failure, public anger, political mandate
- Actual outcome: bailouts, QE, finance sector larger within 5 years
- Proved bailout reflex stronger than reform impulse
- Most important single data point for this question

**Mechanisms that could substitute for collapse:**
- Geopolitical shock (Taiwan crisis, war) — defence necessity bypasses ideology
- Technological disruption (AI displacing financial sector jobs)
- Gradual dollar erosion forcing adjustment
- Democratic insurgency (Sanders/Corbyn model — both defeated)

**Most likely trajectory:** Managed decline + periodic crises + insufficient
reform = slow suffocation while competitive gap grows.

## 6. Vested Interests Blocking Reform

### Financial Sector Core
- Major banks (JPMorgan, Goldman, Citi, BofA, HSBC, Deutsche)
- Asset managers (BlackRock ~$10tn, Vanguard, State Street)
- Hedge funds and private equity (Blackstone, KKR, Carlyle, Bridgewater)
- Insurance and derivatives complex

### Real Estate / Property
- Landlord class and REITs benefiting from zoning-restricted supply
- Housing as investment asset class vs social good
- Land value appreciation without productive contribution (George's insight)

### Corporate Monopolies
- Big Tech platform rent (Apple/Google 30% app store fees)
- Pharma patent rent on publicly funded research
- Defence contractors on cost-plus contracts
- Telecoms/utilities charging for decades-old infrastructure

### Political Infrastructure
- Campaign finance (Citizens United) — unlimited corporate political spending
- Lobbying (~$4bn/year officially, finance consistently top spender)
- Both parties structurally captured — not partisan, systemic
- Gilens & Page (2014): elite preferences win ~100% vs public on economic policy

### Intellectual Infrastructure
- Think tanks (Cato, Heritage, Peterson Institute, Brookings)
- Economics journals — heterodox approaches excluded
- Academic chairs endowed by financial institutions
- Rating agencies (S&P, Moody's, Fitch) — paid by issuers they rate

### The Revolving Door (Core Mechanism)
- Treasury ↔ Goldman Sachs ↔ Treasury (Rubin, Paulson, Mnuchin)
- Regulators drawn from and return to regulated industry
- Future income depends on not writing rules that work
- Legal, not corrupt — rational self-interest within captured system

## 9. Open Source Crypto as Alternative System

**The case for:**
- Trustless settlement without rent-seeking intermediaries
- Permissionless access — banking the unbanked
- Programmable money replacing financial middlemen
- Censorship resistance — genuine monetary sovereignty
- Non-dollar settlement infrastructure between nations

**The problems:**
- Scalability (Bitcoin: ~7 tx/sec vs Visa: ~24,000)
- Volatility — cannot be unit of account for industrial planning
- Governance — decentralization recentralizes around developers/whales
- Wealth concentration — more unequal than dollar system among early holders
- Self-custody complexity — most users will use custodians, recreating problem

**The capture problem:**
- BlackRock Bitcoin ETF — Wall St absorbing crypto, not being displaced
- DeFi largely reproduced financial capitalism's pathologies
- Technology can replace rent-seeking intermediaries; political economy
  determines whether it will be allowed to

**Most plausible role:**
- Transitional infrastructure for non-dollar nation-to-nation settlement
- Parallel system that grows alongside existing system until old one obsolete
- Not political victory over dollar system but making it irrelevant by use case

## 10. The Eastern Model Winning by Default

**The structural argument:**
Every hegemonic transition involved the rising power having superior productive
capacity. US displaced UK because it made more things better and cheaper.
China is doing the same to the West.

**The deepest irony:**
> The Eastern model wins not because it is a better form of capitalism but
> because it maintained political primacy over capital — what Western
> democracies surrendered to financial interests over 50 years.

**Eastern model vulnerabilities (genuine):**
- Debt-driven overinvestment (property sector collapse)
- Demographic cliff — rapidly aging population
- Innovation ceiling — strong at scaling, weaker at frontier breakthroughs
- Political brittleness — concentrated power amplifies policy errors
- Domestic consumption weakness — export/investment dependent growth

**Why it still probably wins:**
- Western vulnerabilities are structural and self-reinforcing
- Reform blocked by the very interests that created the problem
- No political mechanism exists to overcome this without crisis
- By the time crisis forces reform, competitive gap may be uncloseable

## Open Questions

   to close the gap before it becomes structural?

   existing financial interests?

3. Does China's political model contain the seeds of its own failure — can
   concentrated power without democratic accountability sustain long-term
   adaptive capacity?

   a trigger event (Taiwan, Gulf pivot, debt crisis) make it acute?
